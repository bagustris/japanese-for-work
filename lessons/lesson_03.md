# Lesson 3: 私のおすすめは、墨田区の水族館です。

#3
Expressing an opinion concisely
私のおすすめは、墨田区の水族館です。
I recommend the aquarium in Sumida.
ごめん、ちょっといいかな。
Do you have a minute?
In this case, a superior is speaking to a subordinate. 「ごめん」is a casual way to let a person know that you need some of their time.
For a subordinate asking for a superior&apos;s time, use: 「すみません。ちょっといいですか」
はい、何でしょうか。
Yes, what is it?
This is a polite response to a superior. 「はい、何ですか」 may be seen as a bit blunt. 「～でしょうか」 is commonly used in a professional environment.
If you change「書類は何部必要ですか」(How many copies do you need?) to 「必要でしょうか」, the question sounds much more polite.
Try using 「～でしょうか」instead of 「～ですか」when speaking to superiors or clients. When in doubt, listen to what phrases the people around you are using.
君は確か、水族館に詳しかったよね。
You know a lot about aquariums, right?
Using the phrase「確か〜（た）よね」is a way to check your memory of something.
「Tomanik さんは確かポーランド出身だったよね」→ Tomanik-san, you&apos;re from Poland, right?
「確か上野にも水族館があったよね」→ There&apos;s an aquarium in Ueno, right?
はい、よく行きますが。
Yes, I go pretty often…
The speaker is wondering why they are being asked this question.
「はい、よく行きますが、そのことについて何かありますか（どうしましたか）」→ Yes, I go pretty often, but why are you asking me?
It is common in Japanese for only the first half of a phrase to be used. By adding 「が」 , the speaker is wondering aloud why they are being asked, and prompting the other person to continue what they were saying.
水族館だったら、どこがいいかな。
What aquarium do you recommend?
This phrase 「～だったら、どこがいいかな」(What ~ do you recommend?) is used by superiors to subordinates, or among equals, when a recommendation is needed.
For a subordinate asking a superior for a recommendation, use:「～でしたら、どこがいいでしょうか」
私のおすすめは、墨田区の水族館です。
I recommend the aquarium in Sumida.
This form of recommendation is very common.
「私のおすすめは、～です」 → I recommend the ~.
You can change the order like this:
「墨田区の水族館が私のおすすめです」→ My recommendation is the aquarium in Sumida.
In this case, use「～が」instead of 「～は」to add emphasis.
In the business world, it&apos;s important to lead with a conclusion. These phrases are commonly used:
「この製品の特長は〜です」→ This product&apos;s advantage is ~.
「調査からわかったことは〜です」→What we learned from the research is ~.
Sentences using the「～は～です」structure are very simple. Try using this structure to lead with a conclusion first, then provide details.
Kanji: 私　「し」、私用　「しよう」　personal matter, 公
私
「こうし」public and private
墨田区か。それはどうして。
Sumida Aquarium. Why&apos;s that?
As a written sentence,「どうして墨田区ですか」would be more appropriate. As a spoken sentence, the structure may change as above. The speaker can arrange the sentence so that the most important part is spoken first, like this:
「詳しいんだね、水族館のこと」→ You know a lot about aquariums.
「ありがとうね、いろいろ教えてくれて」→ Thanks for everything you have told me.
理由は二つあります。
I have two reasons.
When listing reasons, explanations tend to get long. By letting the listener know in advance what to expect, you can make your answer sound logical.
まず一つ目の理由は、900種類以上の魚が見られることです。
Reason one, it has more than 900 types of fish.
This expression is used when sharing the first reason. Insert what you wish into the following template to make an explanation.
「一つ目の理由は～ことです」→ The first reason is ~.
「珍しい魚が多いことです」→ There are lots of rare fish.
「貴重な魚が見られることです」→ You can see lots of valuable fish.
二つ目の理由は、夜遅くまで開いていて、会社の帰りにも行けることです。
Reason two, it&apos;s open late, so I can go on the way home from work.
Reasons can be counted as 一つ目 (first)、二つ目 (second)、and 三つ目 (third).
When there are lots of reasons, say:
「理由はいろいろありますが、主な理由は三つあります」→ There are many reasons, but here are the main three.
This creates a simpler explanation.
それはいいね。
That&apos;s great.
いい情報をありがとう。
Thanks for the information.
Rather than simply saying「ありがとうございます」, in this case the speaker is thanking the other person for something specific.
どういたしまして。
You&apos;re welcome.
Here are some other phrases you can use:
「いえいえ」→ Not at all.
「いえ、お役に立てたなら嬉しいです」→ I&apos;m glad I could help.

It&apos;s important to make concise explanations in the business world. Keep the listener&apos;s feelings in mind, and keep explanations short. Watch out for phrases like 「・・・して、・・・して」. They allow the speaker to keep going endlessly! Remember to be concise and keep sentences short.
Aim to lead with the conclusion. When providing reasons, try to let the listener know in advance how many reasons to expect. You can use the following phrases:
「理由は二つあります。一つ目の理由は～」 → I have two reasons. The first is ~.
「二つ目の理由は～」→ The second is ~.
