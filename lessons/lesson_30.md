# Lesson 30: 今はちょっと手が離せません。終わり次第お手伝いします。

#30
Turning down a request for support politely
今はちょっと手が離せません。終わり次第お手伝いします。
I&apos;m in the middle of something. I&apos;ll help you as soon as I&apos;m done.
すみません、２番テーブルのお客様の前菜はまだですか。
Excuse me, where are the appetizers for Table 2?
「前菜はまだですか」means 「前菜はまだできていませんか」(Are the appetizers still not ready?). The speaker is wondering when the appetizers will be ready. The phrase「頼んだコピー、まだですか」(Where are the copies I asked for?) uses a similar structure to check on the status of a request. However, this is a very direct means of questioning, so be careful when using this phrase.
今ちょうど盛りつけているところです。
I&apos;m plating them now.
「ところ」is often used to mean &quot;place,&quot; but in this case, it&apos;s being used to mean 「まさに今」(right now). It can be used in phrases like these to show your current progress on a task:「今から盛りつけるところ」(I&apos;m about to plate them.),「ちょうど盛りつけているところ」(I&apos;m plating them now.) and「たった今、盛りつけたところ」(I just finished plating them.).
ワンさん、このあと１番テーブルはデザートになりますけど、できていますか。
Wang-san, Table 1 is going to need their desserts. Are they done?
Here, 「できていますか」is being used to mean「出来上がっていますか」. The phrase「〜になる」is being used to tell the listener what is coming up next. At this restaurant, which serves multi-course meals, 前菜 (appetizers) are followed by 主菜 (main dish), which is followed by デザート(dessert).
まだです。あともう少しかかります。
Not yet. I need a little more time.
The phrase 「もう少しかかります」 means「もう少し時間がかかる」, but omits「時間が」.
皆さん、お客様をお待たせしないようお願いしますね！
We can&apos;t keep the customers waiting, guys!
The speaker is trying to rally the kitchen to work as fast as possible so that customers are not kept waiting much longer. Adding「ね」at the end of the sentence is used for emphasis.
はい！
Okay!
これじゃあ、全然人手が足りませんね。
We&apos;re understaffed!
「人手」, or worker, means the same thing as「働く人」or「働き手」.
一気に５組も団体さんが入ったら追いつかないですよ。
If five groups come in at once like this, we can&apos;t keep up!
「一気に」means the same thing as「一度に」(at once). The word「団体さん」refers to「グループ客」, or a group. The word「追いつかない」is used to mean that one can&apos;t keep up or cope. It can be used in sentences like these: 「対応が追いつかない」(Can&apos;t keep up with responding)「サービスが追いつかない」(Can&apos;t provide service as fast as required)「コロナ患者が急増し処置が追いつかない」(Can&apos;t cope with the rising number of coronavirus cases). This can often come into play when the situation exceeds what is normal, or typical of comparable situations in the past.
ワンさんは大丈夫そうですか。
Wang-san, how are you doing?
The speaker is checking on their coworker. The phrase「〜そうです」refers to how things look, or how things seem to be shaping up. Though the speaker is busy, they are checking on Wang-san and asking if he will be okay.
なんとか大丈夫です。
I should be fine.
「なんとか」is a word that&apos;s used to show one is getting through a difficult situation. For example,「なんとか行けそうです」(I should be able to manage to go.) or「なんとかなりそうです」(It should work out somehow.).
2番テーブルのお客様から「前菜を10分以上待っている」とクレームが入りました。
Table 2 has been waiting for appetizers for over ten minutes! They&apos;re not happy.
Here, the speaker uses the word 「入りました」instead of just「クレームがありました」to show that a complaint has just come in, and to create a sense of urgency. 「電話が入った」and「メールが入った」are other examples of this sense of urgency being created through 「入りました」.
大至急仕上げてください！
I need the appetizers now!
「至急」means「大急ぎ」. Adding the kanji 「大」to make「大至急」creates an even greater sense of urgency. The word「仕上げる」means「完成させる」.
ワンさん、デザートもそろそろですから！
Wang-san, I&apos;ll need the desserts soon!
「そろそろ出すことになる」explains that it is nearly time for the desserts to be brought out. The implication is that the speaker wants Wang-san to hurry up and finish the desserts. If the speaker ended the sentence with「そろそろです」, it would just serve as a friendly update
about the time. However, by adding 「から」to make「そろそろですから」, we can understand the hidden implication.
「そろそろ」is often used in gentler phrases like 「そろそろ行きましょう」and「そろそろ帰りましょう」, but in this case, it&apos;s being used to prompt a listener to action.
あー、どうしよう！まずい、間に合わないよ…。
Argh! What do I do? I can&apos;t make these fast enough…
ワンさん、ちょっとだけこっちの仕事を手伝ってくれませんか。
Wang-san, could you help me out a bit?
「こっちの仕事」is being used to refer to what the speaker is working on. The speaker wants help with their job making appetizers, because they are overwhelmed with work. The phrase「～てくれませんか」is used by superiors to subordinates, or among coworkers. When asking a superior to help with something, use 「～てもらえませんか」or「～ていただけませんか」.
すみません、今はちょっと手が離せません。終わり次第お手伝いします。
I&apos;m in the middle of something. I&apos;ll help you as soon as I&apos;m done.
「手が離せない」means「今目の前に先にやるべき仕事がある」, and that the speaker is busy with their own work. The phrase「終わり次第」means「終わったらすぐに」, but is more polite, and more appropriate for use in a business situation. It lets the listener know that the speaker will help them as soon as they are able to, and that the listener&apos;s work will become their top priority.
そうですか…。じゃあ、終わったらお願いします。
I see. Well, as soon as you&apos;re done!
In this case, 「終わったら」isn&apos;t being used to mean &quot;if you finish,&quot; but instead &quot;when you finish.&quot; It means the same thing as「終わってから」or「終わった後で」. Here, the sentence has the same meaning as「その仕事が終わったらこちらを手伝ってください」; in other words, the speaker is asking for help as soon as the listener&apos;s task is done.
分かりました。
Okay.
じゃあ、私が手伝いましょうか。
Shall I help?
The chef in charge of main dishes is offering help. The phrase「〜ましょうか」can be used in phrases like「私がやりましょうか」or「何かしましょうか」to offer help.
ありがとうございます！
Thank you!
Kanji: 務「きん」、出勤「しゅっきん」go to work, 勤務「きんむ」work duty, 務める
Keigo:
行きます→
参ります（まいります）
Words from this Dialogue
前菜「ぜんさい」
appetizers
The kanji「菜」can be used to refer to a side dish. 「前菜」, or appetizers, are what precede a main dish. Multi-course meals may include 「前菜」 (appetizers)、「スープ」 (soup)、「メイン」(魚料理、肉料理）(main dishes, including meat or fish)、and 「デザート」 (dessert).
「前菜盛り合わせ」を頼み、みんなで分けて食べることにした。
「ぜんさいもりあわせをたのみ、みんあでわけてたべることにした」
We decided to share the appetizer platter.
前菜の量が多くて、それだけでお腹がいっぱいになりそうだ。
「ぜんさいのりょうがおおくて、それだれでおなかがいっぱいになりそうだ」
The appetizers look big enough to fill me up.
人手「ひとで」
workers, help, labor
This term is used to refer to workers or help, or the labor force in general.
深刻な人手不足が続いていて、人手を確保しようとする企業が増えている。
「しんこくなひとでぶそくがつずいていて、ひとでをかくほしようとするきぎょうがふえている」
There is a serious labor shortage, and many companies are trying to secure workers.
企業は、人手不足解消に向けて議論を続けている。
「きぎょうは、ひとでぶそくかいしょうにむけてぎろんをつずけている」
The company is continuing to hold discussions about how to solve the labor shortage.
団体さん「だんたいさん」
group
A 団体 is a group of people gathered for a specific purpose. By adding the suffix「さん」on the end, it becomes more polite and friendly.
Groups of customers can be collectively called 「団体さん」.
今日チェックインする団体さんは、何名かな。
「きょうちぇっくいんするだんたいさんは、なんめいかな」
How many people are in the group that&apos;s checking in later today?
観光バスから団体さんが降りてきた。
「かんこうばすだんたいさんがおりてきた」
A group of people got off of the tour bus.
